# Legal Contemporary Monologue Sources

## ‚öñÔ∏è Legal Summary

**Question**: Can we display excerpts/portions of copyrighted contemporary monologues?

**Answer**: **NO** - Not for commercial SaaS use.

Even small excerpts don't qualify for fair use when:
- Your platform is commercial (subscription-based)
- The work is creative in nature (plays/scripts)
- You're using the "heart" of the work (monologues are the essence)
- You compete with the original market (monologue discovery platform)

## ‚úÖ Legal Sources for Contemporary Monologues

### 1. Project Gutenberg (1920s-1960s)

Works published between 1928-1964 that didn't renew copyright are public domain.

**Available Collections:**
- [Contemporary One-Act Plays (1922)](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/37970) - B. Roland Lewis et al.
- Fifty Contemporary One-Act Plays (1920) - Frank Shay & Pierre Loving
- One-Act Plays by Modern Authors (1921) - Helen Louise Cohen

**How to find more:**
1. Search Project Gutenberg for plays published 1920-1964
2. Look for theater anthologies from that era
3. Focus on American plays (easier to verify copyright status)

### 2. Creative Commons Licensed Plays

**Sources:**
- [Instant Monologues](https://www.instantmonologues.com/) - Original CC-licensed contemporary monologues
- [Stage Partners](https://www.yourstagepartners.com/resources/free-monologues) - Free contemporary monologues (verify CC license)
- Independent playwrights who explicitly license work under CC

**Verify before scraping:**
- Confirm CC license type (CC-BY, CC-BY-SA, etc.)
- Respect attribution requirements
- Check for commercial use restrictions

### 3. Post-1964 Public Domain Works

Works where copyright explicitly lapsed or was dedicated to public domain:
- Some government-funded educational plays
- Works explicitly released to public domain by authors
- Rare, but they exist

## üö´ What We CANNOT Do

### Illegal Sources:
1. **Modern plays without permission** (post-1978 works are copyrighted for life + 70 years)
2. **"Fair use" excerpts** - Fair use doesn't apply to:
   - Commercial platforms
   - Substantial portions (monologues are substantial)
   - Market substitution (we're a monologue platform)
3. **Scripts from movies/TV** - Almost always copyrighted
4. **Broadway/Off-Broadway plays** - Unless explicitly licensed
5. **Samuel French/Dramatists Play Service** - Copyrighted catalog

### Common Misconceptions:
- ‚ùå "It's just a portion" - Doesn't matter for commercial use
- ‚ùå "It's for education" - Our platform is commercial
- ‚ùå "We're helping promote the work" - Not a legal defense
- ‚ùå "We link to buy the full script" - Doesn't grant excerpt rights

## üé≠ Bottom Line

**What IS legal:**
- Public domain works (pre-1928, or 1928-1964 without renewal)
- CC-licensed contemporary works with proper attribution
- Works explicitly released to public domain

**What is NOT legal:**
- Any copyrighted work without explicit permission
- "Fair use" excerpts on a commercial platform
- Scraping from unauthorized sources

**When in doubt:**
Assume it's copyrighted. The legal risk isn't worth it for a growing SaaS business.
